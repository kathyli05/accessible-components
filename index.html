Introduction

As our world becomes increasingly digital and user interface design becomes more complex, it is important that all interfaces be designed with accessibility in mind. However, many web components (e.g. buttons, dropdowns, checkboxes) are not equally accessible for users on different devices and with different physical/mental needs.

This motivated me to investigate checkboxes - a seemingly simple but deceptively nuanced UI component - and investigate how their inputs and outputs vary across three different platforms. With these insights, I then redesigned one of the platform's checkboxes to better account for inclusive and intuitive design.

A few notes:

  • I use a Thinkpad X14 Carbon and an iPhone 14 for the mobile versions. For my laptop browser, I use Google Chrome, and mobile browser, I use Safari.
  • I use the word "toggle" to describe the checking or unchecking of a checkbox.

Applications Overview

I chose to investigate three platforms for my case study - Google Forms, Microsoft To-do, and the video game Hades. These three platform's checkboxes are all used in different contexts, which I figured was useful in comparing their usability.

Input Interactions

After a basic test of each component and understood the various states each component could be used in, I further investigated each component's inputs and how they performed in four key aspects of good UI design: learnability, memorability, efficiency, and accessibility. When interacting with a checkbox, inputs refer to the different ways a user can engage with the component, such as clicking with a mouse, using keyboard shortcuts, or tapping on a touchscreen. Thus, I explored how users interact with each applications' checkboxes across mouse, keyboard, and mobile inputs.

    Output Feedback

    When a user interacts with a checkbox, the component should provide clear output signals to reflect its current state. These outputs serve as confirmation of an action and are essential for usability and accessibility. Outputs can include visual indicators, auditory cues, or haptic feedback. They may also be more subtly implemented, such as screen reader announcements or focus outlines. Thus, I proceeded by analyzing the outputs for each application.

    Redesign

    Visualizing the interaction flow with these models helped me identify usability and accessibility issues. Thus, I chose to change one aspect of the component to address these issues - adding a clearer hover/focus indicator to the entire checkbox and its label. Previously, only the checkbox itself had an indicator, with a much lighter color. This was an intentional trade-off I made, affecting learnability, memorability, efficiency, and accessibility for mouse and keyboard users.

    Reflection

    My design addresses two accessibility gaps: the almost-hidden hover/focus indicator in Google Forms mouse/keyboard navigation and the lack of visual alignment between interactive areas and visual feedback. This change solves the "mismatch" described by Kat Holmes—there is a disconnect between the ability to interact with the entire label row and the visual indicator that only appears on hover/focus, creating unnecessary precision challenges for users.

    From my observations, Microsoft To-do’s small checkbox hitbox may negatively impact users with motor impairments who rely on touch input, while Hades' multimodal feedback (audio, visual, haptic) will significantly benefit users with various impairments. Similarly, Google Forms' lack of obvious keyboard focus indicators may create barriers for visually impaired users relying on keyboard navigation.

    In component design, it seems that mouse users are most commonly prioritized, followed by touch/mobile, and then followed by keyboard users. This hierarchy impacts accessibility - keyboard users frequently encounter minimal focus indicators and inconsistent focus navigation, touch users face small targets, and screen reader users may receive inconsistent announcements. This may be frustrating and leave them with an incomplete experience. My redesign attempts to rebalance this by improving visibility for all input methods without adding complexity.

    Conclusion

    I learned a lot about accessible design through my analysis and redesign of the checkbox component. For one, I've realized how important it is to account for learnability, memorability, and efficiency when designing interfaces. Additionally, I've realized that these small usability issues such as a faint hover indicator or non-multimodal forms of feedback affect all users of all abilities, not just a specific group. Ultimately, accessibility is not about perfection (as seen through the tradeoff I made), but rather intention and careful design.
